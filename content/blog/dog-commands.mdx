## Why Dog Commands Are Important

Teaching your dog commands is not just about ensuring obedience—it’s about enhancing your bond and keeping them safe in various situations. Mastering basic dog commands can prevent dangerous situations like running into traffic, eating harmful objects, or getting into conflicts with other dogs.

Commands also help mentally stimulate your dog, which reduces anxiety, boredom, and destructive behavior. By incorporating regular training sessions, your dog can develop discipline and self-control.

#### Directions:

1. Hold a treat in your hand and place it close to your pup’s nose.
2. Move the treat up and command your dog to “sit.”
3. Once he sits, say “Good boy.”
4. Give him the treat.
5. Praise him for following the command.* *

### 2. Down

The “Down” command is taught very similarly to the “Sit” command. But, the benefits are well worth it. It’s a more difficult [command to master](https://www.thedoghouseps.com/canine-emotions-and-how-to-identify-them/), but a great way to calm an anxious or scared dog.

#### Directions:

1. Place your pup’s favorite treat in the palm of your hand and close it.
2. Place your hand up to the dog’s snout. When your pup smells it, move your hand to the ground to make him follow.
3. Once he lay’s down, say, “Down.”
4. Praise him for being a good boy by giving him the treat.

### 3. Stay

Often, the two [commands](https://www.familyeducation.com/life/pet-training/five-essential-commands-your-dog), “Sit”  and “Stay,” go hand-in-hand. Because of this, before you teach your furry friend to stay, make sure he has mastered the ‘sit’ command.

#### Directions:

1. First, command your dog to “Sit.”
2. Then, face the palm of your hand in front of you and say, “Stay.”
3. Maintain eye contact with your dog as you gradually step backward.
4. If your pup stays after a few steps, reward him with a treat.
5. Continue to step further back and reward your dog every couple of steps.
6. Practice this routine over time to see how long your dog can stay. Make it into a fun challenge!

Remember: This command requires lots of self-control from your dog, and some are better than others. The important part is that you don’t give up hope too soon!

After all, great dogs come to those who wait. Cheesy, I know, but it was just too easy.

### 4. Come

After you [teach the “Stay” command](http://www.familytime.com/ShowArticle.Aspx?ArticleId=286), “Come” is the next best thing. A great way to introduce the “Come” command is through leash training your pup.

#### Directions:

1. Place the collar and leash on your dog and walk away from him. Tell him to “Stay.”
2. Then, ask him to “Come” toward you while tugging on the leash gently.
3. If your dog comes to you, reward him with a treat, praise, and affection.

This command is crucial to teach your dog for safety reasons. It can be scary if your dog chases cars or pedestrians on the street. The “Come” command teaches your dog to listen and respect your wishes.

### 5. Leave it

![dog training by instructors at our salon](https://www.thedoghouseps.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/WJS_5329-300x200.jpg)This command can help protect your dog from sniffing a dangerous object that could cause harm.

#### Directions:

1. Place a treat in both your hands.
2. Open the hand with the treat inside.
3. When your dog tries to eat the treat, say, “Leave it.”
4. If your dog stops, give him the treat from your other hand.
5. If he tries to lick, paw, or bark to get the treat — ignore his behaviors.
6. Repeat until your pup steps away from the first fist when you say, “Leave it.”
7. Next, give your dog the treat only when he moves away from the first fist and looks up at you.

## Additional Dog Commands to Teach for Advanced Training

Once your dog has mastered the five essential commands, you can introduce more advanced dog commands that build on their obedience skills. These commands help with their mental development and make everyday activities more manageable.

### Heel

The “heel” command is essential for walks and maintaining control when your dog is on a leash. It teaches your dog to stay close to you while walking, reducing pulling and ensuring they don’t get distracted by external factors.

**How to Teach It:**

- Use a leash to guide your dog close to your side.
- Command “heel” and reward them when they stay close.
- Gradually increase the distance and time spent walking at your side.

### Wait

Similar to “stay,” the “wait” command is slightly different as it signals your dog to pause until you give them the go-ahead. This is useful when entering or exiting doors or crossing busy streets.

**How to Teach It:**

- Command your dog to sit or stand and say “wait.”
- Reward them after a few seconds of waiting.
- Practice with gradual increases in time before rewarding.

## Positive Reinforcement: The Key to Successful Training

When teaching dog commands, positive reinforcement is crucial for success. Using treats, praise, and affection when your dog performs commands correctly strengthens their motivation to obey. It’s important to be consistent in your rewards and training routines, as this helps solidify their understanding of each command.

Remember, training should be fun for both you and your dog. Short, frequent sessions are more effective than long, overwhelming ones. Over time, your dog will respond more naturally to each command.

## Common Mistakes in Teaching Dog Commands

While teaching dog commands can be rewarding, some common mistakes can hinder progress. Being aware of these mistakes can help you adjust your training methods for better results.

1. **Inconsistency**: Switching between different cues or allowing your dog to get away with ignoring commands can cause confusion. Make sure you’re consistent with the words and hand signals you use.
2. **Punishing Mistakes**: Dogs learn best through positive reinforcement. If they make a mistake, avoid punishment, and instead use it as an opportunity to guide them correctly.
3. **Lack of Patience**: Training takes time. If your dog doesn’t learn a command right away, don’t give up. Stay patient and consistent in your approach.
