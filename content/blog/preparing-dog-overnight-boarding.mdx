## Preparing Your Dog Mentally

The mental preparation for boarding starts weeks in advance:

**Desensitization to New Environments:**
- Take your dog to new places regularly (pet stores, parks, friends' houses)
- Practice leaving your dog briefly with a trusted person
- Visit the boarding facility ahead of time if possible
- Allow your dog to explore and become comfortable with the space

**Crate Training:**
- Even if your dog doesn't regularly sleep in a crate, boarding facilities often use them
- Introduce crate training 4-6 weeks before boarding
- Make the crate positive with treats, toys, and praise
- Practice short stays in the crate

**Socialization with Other Dogs:**
- Attend dog parks or daycare if your dog will be in a group environment
- Practice meeting new dogs in controlled settings
- Address any reactive or aggressive behaviors with a trainer before boarding

**Routine Stability:**
- Maintain consistent feeding, walking, and play schedules
- Sudden changes in routine before boarding increase stress
- Keep your tone calm and matter-of-fact about upcoming boarding

**Leash Walking:**
- Ensure your dog is comfortable being walked by someone other than you
- A staff member will need to walk your dog—they should be comfortable with this

## Vaccination and Health Requirements

Texas and Houston facilities (including The Dog House) require specific vaccinations before overnight boarding. This protects your dog and all dogs in the facility:

**Required Vaccinations (Most Common):**
- **DHPP (Distemper, Hepatitis, Parvo, Parainfluenza):** Must be current (within 3 years)
- **Rabies:** Must be current (within 1-3 years depending on vaccine given)
- **Bordetella (Kennel Cough):** Usually required, typically given 3-4 weeks before boarding; discuss timing with your vet
- **Influenza (Dog Flu/H3N2):** Increasingly required; two doses initially, then annual boosters

**Pre-Boarding Vet Check:**
- Schedule a vet visit 2-3 weeks before boarding
- Discuss your dog's behavioral anxiety with the vet
- Address any health concerns (skin conditions, ear issues, digestive problems)
- Ensure vaccinations are current and get documentation

**Health Paperwork:**
- Bring vaccination records to boarding check-in
- Have a list of medical conditions and current medications
- Include your vet's name, phone number, and emergency vet information
- Provide any allergy information

**Special Health Considerations:**
- Older dogs might need additional support or modified activities
- Senior dogs sometimes need medication before their appointment
- Discuss chronic conditions (arthritis, diabetes) with staff

## What to Pack for Boarding

Create a complete packing list for your dog's stay:

**Comfort Items:**
- Dog bed or blanket that smells like home
- Favorite toy (sturdy and safe ones only)
- Chew items (bully sticks, Kong toys)
- Something with your scent (old t-shirt)

**Clothing (if needed):**
- Sweater for small dogs (if they're used to wearing it)
- Rain jacket if weather-appropriate
- Do NOT pack unnecessary clothing that increases laundry

**Feeding Supplies:**
- 7-10 days worth of food in original bags or containers
- Label clearly with your dog's name
- Include feeding instructions (amount, frequency, any mixing)
- Note any dietary sensitivities

**Medical Supplies:**
- All medications in original bottles with clear labels
- Written instructions for medication administration (time, dosage, with/without food)
- Any medical equipment needed (eyedrops, supplements)
- Copy of medical documentation for chronic conditions

**Documentation:**
- Copy of vaccination records
- Emergency contact information
- Vet contact information (your regular vet AND emergency vet)
- Behavioral notes and special instructions
- Photo of your dog (in case of escape, though unlikely)
- Microchip information and registry documentation

**Optional But Helpful:**
- Recent photo of your dog
- Written description of your dog's behavior and personality
- Favorite treat type (in moderation)
- Calming supplements your dog uses at home

## Dietary and Medication Information

Getting diet and medication right is essential for your dog's health during boarding:

**Feeding Instructions:**
- Write clearly: amount, frequency, and specific time of day if relevant
- Note if your dog eats better with hand-feeding or in a crate
- Mention any foods to avoid (allergies, sensitivities)
- Include instructions for water frequency (some dogs need monitored water)
- Note if your dog needs probiotics or digestive enzymes

**Medications:**
- Bring all medications in original pharmacy bottles
- Create a written medication schedule with exact times
- Include reason for medication (for staff understanding)
- Note any side effects or concerns to watch for
- Discuss medication administration at check-in

**Special Diets:**
- If your dog eats prescription diet, bring enough for the entire stay plus extra
- Include vet documentation if the diet is medically necessary
- Bring treats your dog can safely eat
- Note if your dog has difficulty eating (wet food needed, etc.)

**Food Sensitivities:**
- Clearly list any foods that cause digestive upset
- Include symptoms to watch for
- Provide alternative treats that are safe
- Note if your dog has had food-related anxiety issues

## First-Time Boarding Tips

Your dog's first boarding experience sets the tone for future stays:

**Start Small:**
- Consider a short first stay (just overnight) rather than a full week
- Try daycare first to build comfort with the facility and staff
- A weekend overnight lets your dog transition more gradually

**Visit the Facility:**
- Bring your dog to visit before boarding day
- Let them explore, smell, and become familiar with the space
- Introduce your dog to key staff members
- This familiarity significantly reduces stress

**Practice the Goodbye:**
- Don't make departures dramatic or emotional
- Keep it brief and matter-of-fact
- Avoid sneaking out; say goodbye clearly
- A calm, confident departure teaches your dog this is normal

**Choose Off-Peak Times:**
- Avoid boarding during chaotic times (before holidays)
- First stay should be during a calm week at the facility
- Staff can give your dog extra attention when not overwhelmed

**Trust the Process:**
- Your anxiety transmits to your dog
- Boarding facilities do this daily; they know what they're doing
- Your dog will be fine, even if they're sad when you leave

## Managing Separation Anxiety

Some dogs struggle with separation, especially their first time:

**Before Boarding:**
- Work with a trainer on basic separation tolerance
- Practice leaving your dog for increasing lengths of time
- Use positive reinforcement, never force
- Consider calming supplements recommended by your vet
- Discuss anxiety medications with your vet if severe

**At the Facility:**
- Tell staff about your dog's anxiety level
- Ask if they can provide extra attention or monitoring
- Check-in on whether calming techniques have helped
- Some facilities offer anxiety management; ask what's available

**Calming Strategies:**
- Calming treats or supplements before drop-off
- Calming pheromone sprays (Adaptil for dogs)
- Anxiety wraps (Thundershirt) if your dog tolerates them
- Consider short-term anti-anxiety medication prescribed by your vet

**Mental Stimulation:**
- Tired dogs are calmer dogs
- Ask staff to provide play, training, and mental enrichment
- Kong toys with peanut butter or treats provide distraction
- This naturally reduces anxiety

**Recovery Time:**
- Expect your dog to be very tired after boarding
- They may be clingy or need extra attention for a day
- This is completely normal
- Get back to normal routine quickly

## Before Arrival: Final Checklist

The week before boarding, complete these tasks:

**Grooming:**
- Bathe and groom your dog (clean nails, trimmed paw pads)
- Clean ears thoroughly
- Brush thoroughly to remove matting
- Your dog will be more comfortable in the facility

**Exercise:**
- Increase daily exercise 2-3 days before boarding
- A well-exercised dog settles more easily
- Tire them out physically and mentally
- Continue normal exercise routine, don't go overboard

**Vet Check:**
- Get vaccination records if not recently done
- Ask vet about any boarding-related concerns
- Discuss your dog's anxiety level
- Get emergency medication if needed (anti-anxiety meds)

**Documentation Organization:**
- Make copies of all documents
- Organize medications in labeled bags
- Create a written schedule for medications and feeding
- Include emergency contact information prominently

**Pack and Check:**
- Pack everything at least 2 days before
- Double-check all medications are included
- Verify vaccination records are current
- Confirm you have emergency contact numbers

**Communication with Facility:**
- Confirm your check-in time
- Provide any last-minute behavioral updates
- Clarify pick-up times and procedures
- Ask about communication during stay (updates, photos, etc.)

## During Boarding: What to Expect

Understanding what happens during your dog's stay helps ease your mind:

**First Few Hours:**
- Staff will help your dog settle in
- Your dog may be initially stressed (this is normal)
- Most dogs calm down within a few hours
- Extra attention and play help them adjust

**Daily Routine:**
- Feeding at scheduled times
- Multiple potty breaks throughout the day
- Play/socialization (varies by facility type)
- Rest/crate time for calmness
- Individual attention and monitoring

**Monitoring:**
- Staff observe for health issues (vomiting, diarrhea, injury)
- Behavioral changes are noted
- If problems arise, your vet is contacted
- You should receive updates (frequency varies)

**Activities:**
- Daycare or group play (if included)
- Individual attention and training
- Walking and exercise
- Mental enrichment with toys and games
- Grooming/nail care as needed

## FAQ

**Q: Will my dog be traumatized by boarding?**
A: Most dogs adapt quickly, especially with proper preparation. Some seem sad when you leave but bounce back immediately. One or two overnight stays don't cause lasting trauma—inconsistent care or poor conditions do.

**Q: Can I board my puppy?**
A: Generally not until 16+ weeks old (after initial vaccine series). Check with your facility about minimum age requirements.

**Q: My dog has never been boarded. Is she too old to start?**
A: Age isn't an issue; older dogs can handle boarding. Senior dogs need extra attention and potentially modified activities, but boarding is fine at any age.

**Q: What if my dog gets sick during boarding?**
A: Professional facilities have protocols for illness. Your vet will be contacted, and they'll provide care. This is why emergency vet information and good preparation matter.

**Q: Should I worry if my dog seems sad when I pick up?**
A: Post-boarding sadness is normal and temporary. They had a routine interrupted. Give them a day to readjust to normal life. They'll be back to normal soon.
