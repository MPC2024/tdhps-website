## Why Vaccination Requirements Matter

Boarding facilities house multiple dogs in close proximity. This environment is perfect for disease transmission if dogs aren't properly vaccinated. These requirements exist to:

- **Protect all dogs** from contagious diseases
- **Reduce disease outbreaks** in the facility
- **Prevent transmission** to your dog at home
- **Maintain public health** and community safety
- **Reduce facility liability** and risk

Your dog benefits tremendously from being in a vaccinated environment. A facility that doesn't require vaccines is actually riskier for your dog, not easier.

## Core Vaccinations (Required)

Core vaccinations protect against serious, potentially fatal diseases. All reputable boarding facilities require these:

### DHPP (4-in-1 Vaccine)

**Stands for:**
- **D**istemper
- **H**epatitis
- **P**arvoviruses (CPV)
- **P**arainfluenza

**What it protects against:**
- **Canine Distemper:** Highly contagious respiratory disease. Can be fatal, especially in puppies.
- **Hepatitis:** Liver infection that can cause severe illness and death.
- **Parvovirus:** Highly contagious, often fatal disease causing vomiting and diarrhea. Puppies and unvaccinated dogs are at extreme risk.
- **Parainfluenza:** Respiratory virus contributing to kennel cough.

**Vaccination Schedule:**
- **Puppies:** Series of 3-4 vaccines at 6-8 weeks, 10-12 weeks, and 14-16 weeks of age, then booster at 1 year
- **Adults:** Annual or every 3 years depending on vaccine type
- **Boarding requirement:** Must be current (usually within 1-3 years)

**Why it matters for boarding:**
Parvovirus especially is a serious risk in group environments. Unvaccinated dogs can contract it from contaminated areas even after other dogs leave. This vaccine is non-negotiable.

### Rabies Vaccine

**What it protects against:**
A fatal viral infection that affects the nervous system. Once symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal.

**Vaccination Schedule:**
- **Initial:** First rabies vaccine at 12-16 weeks of age
- **Booster:** 1 year after first dose
- **Subsequent:** Every 1-3 years depending on vaccine type (1-year or 3-year vaccine)

**Boarding requirement:**
- Current rabies vaccine required
- Documentation essential (proof of vaccination)
- This is usually legally required in Texas, not just facility requirement

**Why it matters:**
Though rare, rabies is catastrophic. No dog should be at a facility without protection. Additionally, rabies vaccination is required by Texas law for any dog over 4 months old.

## Non-Core Vaccinations (Often Required)

While not universally required by law, many Houston boarding facilities require these because they prevent diseases common in group settings:

### Bordetella Bronchiseptica (Kennel Cough)

**What it protects against:**
Highly contagious respiratory infection causing severe, persistent cough. Called "kennel cough" because it spreads rapidly in group settings.

**Vaccination Schedule:**
- Initial vaccination followed by booster (varies by brand and your vet's protocol)
- Given intranasally (nasal spray) or via injection
- Can be given as early as 6 weeks of age
- Booster typically annual

**Boarding requirement:**
- Increasingly required at Houston boarding facilities
- Often required 3-4 weeks BEFORE boarding to be effective
- Timing is crucial—vaccine needs time to build immunity

**Important note:**
Timing matters with bordetella. The vaccine doesn't reach full effectiveness immediately. Schedule vaccination well ahead of your intended boarding date. If you wait until 1 week before boarding, the vaccination might not be effective yet.

**Why it matters for boarding:**
Kennel cough, while rarely serious in healthy dogs, is incredibly contagious in group settings. A facility with unvaccinated dogs can have an outbreak affecting all residents.

### Canine Influenza Vaccine (H3N2)

**What it protects against:**
Canine influenza virus, a contagious respiratory infection causing coughing, sneezing, and lethargy.

**Vaccination Schedule:**
- Two initial doses, 2-4 weeks apart
- Annual booster
- Can be given at 6+ weeks of age

**Boarding requirement:**
- Increasingly recommended and sometimes required
- More common in larger facilities and daycares
- Check with your specific facility

**Why it matters:**
Unlike human flu, canine flu vaccines don't provide 100% immunity—they reduce severity of disease. However, vaccinated dogs are still protected from serious illness and hospitalization.

## Vaccination Timing and Schedules

**For Puppies:**
Getting a puppy ready for boarding requires planning:

- At 6-8 weeks: First DHPP and bordetella
- At 10-12 weeks: Second DHPP and bordetella booster
- At 12-16 weeks: Rabies vaccine (required by law)
- At 14-16 weeks: Final DHPP booster
- At 16+ weeks with all current vaccines: Eligible for boarding

Some facilities will board puppies with incomplete vaccine series, but this is riskier. It's better to wait until your puppy is fully vaccinated.

**For Adult Dogs:**
If your adult dog's vaccines have lapsed:

1. Schedule a vet appointment immediately
2. Bring the following to the appointment:
   - Vaccination records if available
   - ID and microchip information
   - Health concerns or changes since last vet visit
3. Vet will determine which vaccines are needed
4. Some vaccines require waiting periods before boarding eligibility
5. Plan for vaccines at least 2-4 weeks before boarding

**Important:** Don't skip the veterinary examination. Even if just getting vaccines, a vet check ensures your dog is healthy enough for boarding.

## Special Cases and Exceptions

### Titers vs. Boosters

Some owners use antibody titers (blood tests) to check immunity instead of giving booster vaccines.

- A titer shows whether your dog still has immunity from previous vaccination
- Useful for dogs with vaccine sensitivities or health concerns
- Many facilities don't accept titers; they want documentation of recent vaccination
- Ask your facility about their titer policy before counting on this

### Vaccine-Sensitive Dogs

Some dogs have adverse reactions to vaccines:

- Discuss this with your veterinarian
- Your vet might recommend alternate vaccine schedules
- Some vaccines can be given at different times or different products used
- This is something to address BEFORE boarding is urgent
- Don't wait until 1 week before your planned boarding date

### Elderly Dogs

Senior dogs boarding need current vaccines too:

- Age is not an exemption from vaccination requirements
- Older dogs are sometimes more vulnerable to disease, making vaccination even more important
- Discuss senior health concerns with your vet
- Vaccines can be given as long as your dog is in reasonable health

### Previously Unvaccinated or Feral Dogs

If taking in a rescue dog with unknown vaccine history:

- Start from scratch with a complete vaccine series
- This takes 8-16 weeks depending on age
- Plan accordingly if you need boarding for this dog
- Your vet will guide the appropriate timeline

## Preparing for Boarding: Vaccination Checklist

**8-12 Weeks Before Boarding:**
- [ ] Schedule vet appointment
- [ ] Review vaccination records
- [ ] Confirm what vaccines your dog needs
- [ ] Discuss bordetella timing (if required)

**4-8 Weeks Before Boarding:**
- [ ] Get any needed vaccines
- [ ] Get vaccination record from vet
- [ ] Contact boarding facility with questions
- [ ] Confirm their specific requirements

**2-4 Weeks Before Boarding:**
- [ ] If bordetella needed, ensure it's been 3-4 weeks (if timing required)
- [ ] Schedule vet appointment for any remaining vaccines needed
- [ ] Confirm all required vaccines are current
- [ ] Get official vaccination documentation

**1-2 Weeks Before Boarding:**
- [ ] Make copies of vaccination records
- [ ] Confirm boarding facility received vaccination requirements
- [ ] Bring documentation to check-in
- [ ] Ask facility if they need original vs. copy of records

**Vaccination Documentation:**
Bring:
- Original vaccination records or certified copies
- Veterinary clinic name, address, phone number
- Emergency veterinary clinic information
- Rabies tag or documentation of rabies vaccine
- Any medical conditions or medications list

## What If Your Dog Is Behind on Vaccinations

**If boarding is urgent:**
1. Call your vet immediately
2. Explain you need emergency vaccination
3. Many vets can fit you in same day or next day
4. Some veterinary hospitals have extended hours or weekend availability
5. Get documentation immediately after vaccination

**If certain vaccines can't be given:**
1. Be honest with the boarding facility
2. Ask if they'll accept your dog without all vaccines
3. Some will, some won't—depends on facility policy
4. Don't misrepresent your dog's vaccination status
5. Have a veterinary professional document the reason for inability to vaccinate

**If your dog had a vaccine reaction:**
1. Get documentation from your vet
2. Explain to the boarding facility
3. Ask if they'll make exceptions based on vet documentation
4. Some facilities are flexible; some have strict non-negotiable policies
5. You may need to find a facility that will work with your situation

## FAQ

**Q: Does my indoor dog really need boarding vaccinations?**
A: Yes. Boarding facilities are group environments with disease exposure risk. Indoor status doesn't matter.

**Q: What if the vet didn't give my dog a rabies vaccine?**
A: Ask your vet why. Rabies is legally required in Texas. If your dog had a vaccine reaction, get documentation. This explains exceptions, but facilities still may require documentation.

**Q: How long does the DHPP vaccine last?**
A: It depends on vaccine type. Initial series lasts until booster at 1 year. After that, could be 1-3 years depending on whether your vet uses annual or 3-year vaccine. Check your records.

**Q: Can I board my dog without being fully vaccinated?**
A: Depends on the facility. Some facilities are strict; some have limited availability for under-vaccinated dogs. Don't assume—always ask and be honest about vaccination status.

**Q: When can I board my puppy?**
A: After 16+ weeks of age with complete initial vaccine series. Some facilities may require additional waiting time after vaccines. Ask ahead.
